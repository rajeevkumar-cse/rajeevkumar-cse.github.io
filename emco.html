Evolutionary Multiobjective Combinatorial Optimization

A Tutorial @ GECCO 2009, Montreal

Rajeev Kumar

Abstract

Most discrete optimization problems are essentially multiobjective in nature and shown to be NP-hard. Solutions to such problems are attempted using heuristics and approximation algorithms. A major challenge solving these problems is to capture all the equivalent and diverse solutions at convergence. Evolutionary algorithms (EAs) that do not require much of problem specific knowledge are emerging as a powerful alternative, though, they are unable to effectively explore the solution space. Secondly, in the absence of a known reference set, the user does not get any idea about the convergence. Therefore, hybridization is usually needed to have quality solution sets.

Target Audience

The tutorial is intended for professionals, practicing engineers, researchers, students, and educators working in the application areas of evolutionary algorithms, multiobjective and discrete/combinatorial optimization.

Introduction

Most design problems where even a single cost function or objective value is optimized are often NP-hard. (However, Single objective minimal spanning tree and shortest path problem are among the simplest combinatorial problems for which efficient algorithms exist.) Many such uni-criterion design problems are well studied and many heuristics/methods exist for obtaining exact/approximate solutions in polynomial-time. But, in most real-life applications, design problems generally require simultaneous optimization of multiple and often conflicting objectives, subject to satisfaction of some constraints.

Issues & Challenges

Most such problems abstracted/taken from graph theory are NP-complete in single objective, and NP-hard for multiple objectives; the Pareto-front is unknown. Approximate solutions obtained are not verifiable in polynomial time. It makes it difficult to assess the quality of solutions and performance of the solution strategy over the entire range of the Pareto-front. Therefore, solution strategy to solve such unknown problem using MOEA should address the issues of achieving diversity, convergence, and extent effectively.

Description

Discrete/combinatorial optimization has been an interesting and challenging area to researchers belonging to development of algorithms and operational research (OR) techniques for real-world applications. Most such problems abstracted/taken from graph theory are NP-complete in single objective, and NP-hard for multiple objectives. There are many applications of the problems in communication topology and VLSI design.

For most such problems, there do not exist good heuristics/algorithms, therefore, black-box optimization techniques, e.g., EAs are usually preferred for getting effective solutions. In this tutorial, we will do a quick review of some of the standard combinatorial optimization problems, and then include the techniques and design of operators to effectively solve Evolutionary Multiobjective Combinatorial Optimization (EMCO) problems taken from real-world applications. This would be followed by some case-studies taken from standard problems. This interdisciplinary tutorial provides a practical foundation for:

  • An introduction to combinatorial problems and their multiobjective versions,
  • Problem challenges and solution through Evolutionary Multiobjective Optimization (EMO),
  • Need of designing specialized genetic operators, representation and techniques for embedding of (limited) problem specific knowledge, and
  • Few case studies taken form well known EMCO, and comparison of EMO results with the existing heuristics/approximation algorithms.

Key design considerations to designing operators/representations so as to effectively explore the search space will be looked into. In general, the tutorial is aimed at quickly laying a foundation that can be used as the basis for further study, research and development in this emerging area.
